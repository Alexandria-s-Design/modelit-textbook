Chapter 9: Disease and Immunity

NGSS Standards: MS-LS1-8

Opening Hook

In 1918, the Spanish Flu killed 50 million people worldwide—more than World War I. Today, scientists can model how diseases spread through populations and predict outbreaks before they happen. Understanding disease as a SYSTEM can save millions of lives.

How Disease Spreads

Diseases don't just happen randomly; they follow patterns. Epidemiology is the study of how diseases spread through populations.

Pathogens: The Agents of Disease

A pathogen is any organism that causes disease. Main Types:
  • Virus: Tiny infectious agents that need a host cell to replicate (e.g., Flu, COVID-19)
  • Bacteria: Single-celled organisms that can live anywhere (e.g., Strep throat, E. coli)
  • Fungi & Parasites: Other organisms that can cause illness

Routes of Transmission

How does a pathogen get from one person to another?
  • Direct Contact: Touching, shaking hands
  • Airborne: Coughing, sneezing (droplets)
  • Vector-borne: Carried by insects like mosquitoes (Malaria)
  • Contaminated Food/Water: Eating unsafe food

The Numbers of Spread: R0

Scientists use a number called R0 (pronounced "R-nought") to measure how contagious a disease is.
What does R0 mean?
R0 represents the average number of people one infected person will pass the virus to.
  • R0 < 1: Disease dies out
  • R0 = 1: Disease stays stable (Endemic)
  • R0 > 1: Disease spreads exponentially (Epidemic)
Measles has an R0 of 12-18, making it one of the most contagious diseases!

From Outbreak to Pandemic

  • Epidemic: A sudden increase in cases in a specific area.
  • Pandemic: An epidemic that has spread over several countries or continents.
SIR Model: A classic model used by epidemiologists:
  • Susceptible: People who can get sick
  • Infected: People currently sick and spreading it
  • Recovered: People who had it and are now immune

The Immune System

Your body has a powerful defense system to fight off pathogens. The Immune System is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs.

Lines of Defense

  1. First Line (Barriers): Skin, mucus, stomach acid. These stop pathogens from entering.
  2. Second Line (Innate Immunity): General response. Fever, inflammation, and white blood cells that attack anything foreign.
  3. Third Line (Adaptive Immunity): Targeted response. Your body learns to recognize specific enemies.

Key Players: Antigens and Antibodies

How does your body know what to attack?
  • Antigen: A marker on the surface of a pathogen (like a flag). Your body recognizes it as "foreign."
  • Antibody: A Y-shaped protein produced by your body that locks onto specific antigens to neutralize them.
Lock and Key
Antibodies are specific. An antibody for the flu virus won't work on the chickenpox virus. It's like a key that only opens one lock.

White Blood Cells

These are the soldiers of the immune system. Some "eat" bacteria (macrophages), while others (B-cells and T-cells) coordinate the attack and remember the enemy for next time.

Vaccines and Public Health

We don't just have to wait to get sick. We can prepare our immune systems in advance.

How Vaccines Work

A Vaccine trains your immune system to recognize a pathogen without making you sick.
  • It introduces a harmless version or piece of the pathogen (antigen).
  • Your body produces antibodies and "memory cells."
  • If you encounter the real germ later, your body destroys it instantly. This is Immunity.

Herd Immunity

When enough people are immune to a disease, the disease has nowhere to go. This protects people who cannot get vaccinated (like babies or people with weak immune systems).
Herd Immunity Threshold: The percentage of the population that needs to be immune to stop the spread. For highly contagious diseases like measles, this is over 95%!

Public Health Measures

Besides vaccines, we use other tools to stop spread:
  • Quarantine: Separating sick people from healthy people.
  • Hygiene: Hand washing, sanitation.
Real-World Success Story: Smallpox
Smallpox was a deadly virus that killed millions. Through a massive global vaccination effort, it was declared eradicated in 1980. It is the first human disease to be completely wiped off the Earth!

Antibiotic Resistance

Bacteria are living systems too, and they evolve. This is creating a major problem.

What are Antibiotics?

Antibiotics are medicines that kill bacteria. They transformed medicine, making once-deadly infections treatable.
Note: Antibiotics do NOT work on viruses (colds, flu).

The Rise of Superbugs

When we overuse antibiotics or don't finish our medicine, some bacteria survive. These survivors have mutations that make them resistant. They multiply, creating a population of Resistant bacteria.
Evolution in Action
Antibiotic Resistance is a clear example of natural selection.
  1. Variation exists (some bacteria are naturally tougher).
  2. Selection pressure (antibiotic kills the weak ones).
  3. Survival of the fittest (resistant bacteria survive and reproduce).
To prevent this, only use antibiotics when prescribed by a doctor!

Modeling Disease in Cell Collective

Epidemiologists use computer models to save lives. You can too!

Building an SIR Model

In Cell Collective, you can simulate a population during an outbreak. Key Components:
  • Susceptible Pool: Healthy people.
  • Infected Pool: Sick people.
  • Recovered Pool: Immune people.
Connections:
  • Susceptible people become Infected (Transmission Rate).
  • Infected people become Recovered (Recovery Rate).

Testing Strategies

Once your model is running, try different interventions:
  1. Social Distancing: Reduces the transmission rate.
  2. Masks: Reduces the probability of infection.
  3. Vaccination: Moves people directly from Susceptible to Recovered (bypassing infection!).
Challenge! create a model where a disease has a high R0 but low death rate (like a cold), and another with low R0 but high death rate (like Ebola). Which one is harder to control?

Vocabulary

Pathogen: An organism that causes disease (germ)
Virus: A microscopic infectious agent that replicates only inside living cells
Bacteria: Single-celled microorganisms; some cause disease, many are helpful
Immune System: The body's defense network against infections
Antibody: Protein produced by the immune system to neutralize pathogens
Antigen: A substance that triggers an immune response
Vaccine: A substance used to stimulate the production of antibodies and provide immunity
Immunity: The ability to resist a particular infection
Antibiotic: Medicine that inhibits the growth of or destroys bacteria
Resistance: The ability of bacteria to survive exposure to antibiotics
Epidemic: A widespread occurrence of an infectious disease in a community
Pandemic: An epidemic that has spread across a large region or worldwide
Quarantine: Restriction of movement of people to prevent disease spread
Herd Immunity: Resistance to spread of a disease within a population based on pre-existing immunity of a high proportion of individuals

HANDS-ON ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Disease Transmission Simulation

Learning Objective: Visualize how quickly an infection can spread through a group

Materials: Glow sticks (one per student), one "infected" sticker, dark room

Time Required: 20 minutes

Steps:

  1. Give everyone an uncracked glow stick.
  2. Secretly select one student as "Patient Zero" (crack their glow stick).
  3. Students mingle in a dark room. Every 30 seconds, they must shake hands with someone.
  4. If you shake hands with a glowing person, crack your glow stick (you are now infected).
  5. Track how many rounds it takes for the whole class to glow.
  6. Repeat with "Social Distancing" (stand 6 feet apart, no handshakes) and compare results.
  7. Discussion: How did the behavior change the speed of transmission?

Activity 2: Immune System Diagram

Learning Objective: Map the body's response to a pathogen

Materials: Large poster paper, markers, biology textbook references

Time Required: 45 minutes

Steps:

  1. Draw a cartoon character representing a specific pathogen (e.g., "Flu Virus").
  2. Draw the "Battlefield" (the human body).
  3. Illustrate the First Line of Defense (Skin wall, Mucus moat).
  4. Illustrate the Second Line (Macrophage tanks eating invaders).
  5. Illustrate the Third Line (B-cell factories making Antibody missiles).
  6. Label all parts correctly using vocabulary words.
  7. Present your battle plan to the class!

Activity 3: Vaccine Effectiveness Analysis

Learning Objective: Calculate herd immunity thresholds

Materials: Calculator, worksheets with R0 values for different diseases

Time Required: 30 minutes

Steps:

  1. Learn the formula: Threshold = (1 - 1/R0) × 100.
  2. Calculate the threshold for Polio (R0 = 6). Formula: (1 - 1/6) = 0.83 or 83%.
  3. Calculate for Measles (R0 = 18).
  4. Calculate for Ebola (R0 = 2).
  5. Graph the relationship between R0 and Herd Immunity Threshold.
  6. Analyze: Why is it harder to eliminate Measles than Ebola?
  7. Discussion: What happens if vaccination rates drop below the threshold?
