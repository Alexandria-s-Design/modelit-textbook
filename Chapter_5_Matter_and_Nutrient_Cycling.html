Chapter 5: Matter and Nutrient Cycling

NGSS Standards: MS-LS2-3, MS-PS1-5

Opening Hook

That oxygen atom you just breathed in? It might have been exhaled by a dinosaur 65 million years ago. Matter doesn't disappear—it cycles through Earth's systems endlessly.

The Law of Conservation of Matter

Everything in the universe is made of matter. From the stars in the sky to the cells in your body, it's all stuff that takes up space and has mass.

Matter Cannot Be Created or Destroyed

The Law of Conservation of Matter states that matter cannot be created or destroyed. It can only change form.
Conservation: To save or keep something from being lost or wasted. In science, it means the total amount stays the same.
Real-World Example: Burning Wood When you burn a log in a fireplace, it seems to disappear. But it doesn't! The wood turns into:
  • Ash (solid)
  • Smoke (solid particles)
  • Gases (carbon dioxide and water vapor)
If you captured all the smoke, ash, and gases, they would weigh exactly the same as the original log plus the oxygen used to burn it.

The Water Cycle

Water is constantly moving around the Earth. The same water you drink today has been around for billions of years! This continuous movement is called the Water Cycle.

Key Processes

  • Evaporation: The sun heats up water in rivers, lakes, and oceans, turning it into vapor (gas).
  • Condensation: Water vapor cools down in the air and turns back into liquid droplets, forming clouds.
  • Precipitation: When clouds get heavy, water falls back to Earth as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  • Transpiration: Plants release water vapor from their leaves into the air.
  • Runoff: Water flows over the ground into rivers, lakes, and oceans.
Amazing Fact: Less than 1% of Earth's water is available for humans to drink! Most of it is salty ocean water (97%) or frozen in glaciers (2%).

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is the building block of life. It's in every living thing! The Carbon Cycle describes how carbon moves between living things and the environment.

How Carbon Moves

  • Photosynthesis: Plants take in carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the air to make food.
  • Respiration: Animals (and plants!) release CO₂ back into the air when they breathe and use energy.
  • Decomposition: When living things die, decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break them down, releasing carbon back into the soil and air.
  • Fossil Fuels: Over millions of years, buried plants and animals turn into coal, oil, and natural gas. Burning them releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere.
Reservoir: A place where matter is stored for a long time. Examples: The ocean, the atmosphere, and forests are carbon reservoirs.
Carbon Sinks Forests and oceans are called "Carbon Sinks" because they absorb more carbon than they release. They help keep our planet cool!

The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is a crucial nutrient for all living things. It's a key part of DNA and proteins. Even though 78% of the air is nitrogen gas (N₂), most living things can't use it directly!

Making Nitrogen Usable

  1. Nitrogen Fixation: Special bacteria in the soil (and on roots of some plants like beans) turn nitrogen gas into a form plants can use.
    This is the most critical step! Without these bacteria, plants couldn't grow.
  2. Assimilation: Plants take up nitrogen through their roots. Animals get nitrogen by eating plants.
  3. Decomposition: Decomposers return nitrogen to the soil from waste and dead organisms.
  4. Denitrification: Other bacteria turn nitrogen in the soil back into gas, completing the cycle.

Modeling Nutrient Cycles in Cell Collective

Understanding cycles is easier when you can see them in action. Let's model them!

Building a Cycle Model

In Cell Collective, you can create a model that shows how matter moves between different reservoirs. Key Components:
  • Nodes (Circles): Represent the reservoirs (e.g., Atmosphere, Soil, Plants, Animals).
  • Edges (Arrows): Represent the processes that move matter (e.g., Photosynthesis, Eating, Decomposition).

Questions to Explore with Your Model

  1. What happens if you remove the decomposers from your ecosystem model?
  2. How does cutting down forests affect the carbon cycle?
  3. What happens if you add too much fertilizer (nitrogen) to the soil?
Systems Thinking Tip: Cycles are closed loops. A change in one part of the cycle affects EVERYTHING else in the loop.

Vocabulary

Matter: Anything that has mass and takes up space.
Conservation: The principle that matter is neither created nor destroyed.
Evaporation: The process of turning from liquid into vapor.
Condensation: The conversion of a vapor or gas to a liquid.
Precipitation: Water released from clouds in the form of rain, freezing rain, sleet, snow, or hail.
Transpiration: The exhalation of water vapor through the stomata of plants.
Carbon Cycle: The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged among the biosphere, pedosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere of the Earth.
Photosynthesis: The process by which green plants and some other organisms use sunlight to synthesize foods with the aid of chlorophyll.
Decomposition: The state or process of rotting; decay.
Nitrogen Fixation: The chemical processes by which atmospheric nitrogen is assimilated into organic compounds, especially by certain microorganisms as part of the nitrogen cycle.
Denitrification: The loss or removal of nitrogen or nitrogen compounds.
Nutrient: A substance that provides nourishment essential for growth and the maintenance of life.
Cycle: A series of events that are regularly repeated in the same order.
Reservoir: A supply or source of something.

HANDS-ON ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Water Cycle in a Bag

Learning Objective: Observe the water cycle processes in a closed system

Materials: Ziploc bag, water, blue food coloring, sharpie, tape, sunny window

Time Required: 15 minutes setup + observation over a few hours

Steps:

  1. Draw a sun and clouds on the bag with a sharpie.
  2. Mix water with a drop of blue food coloring (to represent the ocean).
  3. Pour a small amount of water into the bag (don't fill it too much).
  4. Seal the bag TIGHTLY.
  5. Tape the bag to a sunny window.
  6. Observe over time: Watch for "clouds" (condensation) forming at the top and "rain" (precipitation) sliding down the sides.
  7. Explain: How does the sun drive the cycle inside the bag?

Activity 2: Carbon Cycle Board Game

Learning Objective: Understand the complexity of carbon movement

Materials: Dice, station signs (Atmosphere, Plant, Animal, Soil, Fossil Fuels, Ocean), tokens for students

Time Required: 30 minutes

Steps:

  1. Set up stations around the room representing different carbon reservoirs.
  2. Students start as a carbon atom at a specific station.
  3. Roll the die to determine where to go next (e.g., if at "Plant", rolling a 1 might mean "Eaten by Animal", rolling a 2 might mean "Respiration to Atmosphere").
  4. Students record their journey.
  5. Discuss: Did everyone follow the same path? Did anyone get stuck in a reservoir (like Fossil Fuels) for a long time?

Activity 3: Nitrogen Cycle Diagram

Learning Objective: Visualize the path of nitrogen through the ecosystem

Materials: Large paper, markers, diagrams for reference

Time Required: 25 minutes

Steps:

  1. Draw the ground, sky, plants (with roots), and animals.
  2. Label the "Atmosphere" with N₂ gas.
  3. Draw bacteria in the soil and on plant roots.
  4. Use arrows to show the flow of nitrogen:
    • Atmosphere → Soil (Nitrogen Fixation)
    • Soil → Plants (Assimilation)
    • Plants → Animals (Eating)
    • Animals/Plants → Soil (Decomposition)
    • Soil → Atmosphere (Denitrification)
  5. Label each process clearly.
