Chapter 11: Systems in Technology

NGSS Standards: MS-ETS1-1, MS-ETS1-2

Opening Hook

Every technology you use—your phone, the internet, cars, airplanes—is a carefully designed SYSTEM. Engineers think in systems: inputs, outputs, feedback, and interconnections. How would YOU design a better system to solve a real problem?

Engineered vs. Natural Systems

We've learned about natural systems like ecosystems and cells. But humans build systems too! These are called engineered systems.

Similarities and Differences

Both natural and engineered systems have parts that work together.

A System is a group of interacting parts that function as a whole. A Component is one part of a system.
  • Natural Systems: Evolve over time (like a forest). Components are biological or physical.
  • Engineered Systems: Designed by humans with a purpose (like a car). Components are mechanical or digital.
Tech Fact: Many engineered systems mimic nature! This is called biomimicry. For example, airplanes were inspired by birds, and Velcro was inspired by burrs sticking to fur.

Computers as Information Systems

Computers are systems that process information. They follow the same basic pattern as many other systems.

The Information Processing Cycle

  • Input: Data enters the system (keyboard, mouse, microphone).
  • Processing: The CPU "thinks" or calculates.
  • Storage: Data is saved (hard drive, memory).
  • Output: The result is shown (screen, speakers, printer).
An Input is what enters a system. An Output is what leaves a system.

Hardware vs. Software

A computer needs both hardware (the physical parts) and software (the instructions) to work.

An Algorithm is a step-by-step set of instructions for a computer to follow.

Transportation Networks

Transportation systems move people and goods from place to place.

Key Components

  • Infrastructure: Roads, rails, bridges, airports.
  • Vehicles: Cars, trains, planes, ships.
  • Control Systems: Traffic lights, air traffic control, GPS.
Engineering Challenge: Traffic Jams!

Traffic engineers use sensors and algorithms to optimize traffic flow. Smart cities use real-time data to change traffic lights and reduce congestion.

Communication Systems

How do you talk to a friend on the other side of the world? Through a communication system!

From Telegraph to Internet

Communication systems have evolved, but the goal is the same: sending information.

  • Source: The person sending the message.
  • Transmitter: Encodes the message (phone converts voice to digital signal).
  • Channel: The medium the signal travels through (wires, fiber optics, radio waves).
  • Receiver: Decodes the message (phone converts signal back to sound).
  • Destination: The person receiving the message.

Internet and Social Networks

The internet is a massive "network of networks."

A Network is a group of connected systems. A Server is a computer that provides data to other computers. A Router directs traffic between networks.

How the Web Works

When you visit a website, your computer sends a request through routers to a server. The server sends the webpage back to you. This happens in milliseconds!

Bandwidth is the maximum amount of data that can travel through a channel at one time.
Viral Spread: Information on social networks spreads like a virus in a biological system. One person shares with friends, who share with their friends, creating exponential growth!

Design Thinking and Systems Approach

Engineers use a special process to solve problems called Design Thinking.

Design Thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation.

The Engineering Design Process

  1. Empathize: Understand the user's needs.
  2. Define: State the problem clearly.
  3. Ideate: Brainstorm many solutions.
  4. Prototype: Build a rough version of the solution.
  5. Test: Try it out and get feedback.
A Prototype is a preliminary model of something. Iteration means repeating a process to improve the result. Feedback is information about a system's output used to adjust the system.
Design Tip: Fail fast and learn! Engineers expect their first prototypes to have problems. Each failure provides feedback to make the next version better.

HANDS-ON ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Design a Solution to a Problem

Learning Objective: Apply design thinking to solve a real-world problem.

Materials: Paper, pencils, sticky notes, recycled materials (cardboard, tape, bottles)

Time Required: 60 minutes

Steps:

  1. Identify a problem in your school or community (e.g., messy cafeteria, heavy backpacks).
  2. Empathize: Interview classmates about how the problem affects them.
  3. Define: Write a problem statement ("How might we...?").
  4. Ideate: Sketch at least 5 different solutions.
  5. Prototype: Build a quick model of your best idea using recycled materials.
  6. Test: Show your prototype to a friend and ask for feedback.
  7. Iterate: Improve your design based on the feedback.

Activity 2: Map Your School's Systems

Learning Objective: Identify and diagram complex systems.

Materials: Large paper, markers

Time Required: 45 minutes

Steps:

  1. Choose a system in your school (e.g., the cafeteria, the library, the bus system).
  2. Identify the inputs (food, money, students, books).
  3. Identify the outputs (waste, meals served, knowledge).
  4. Map the components and how they connect.
  5. Identify feedback loops (e.g., students complaining about food leads to menu changes).
  6. Draw a diagram showing the flow of materials and information.

Activity 3: Build a Simple Circuit

Learning Objective: Demonstrate input and output in an electrical system.

Materials: Battery (AA or 9V), LED light or small buzzer, wires, switch (or paperclip)

Time Required: 30 minutes

Steps:

  1. Connect one wire from the positive terminal of the battery to the switch.
  2. Connect the other side of the switch to the LED (long leg) or buzzer.
  3. Connect the LED (short leg) or buzzer back to the negative terminal of the battery.
  4. Close the switch to complete the circuit.
  5. Identify parts: Switch is the Input, Electricity is the Flow, Light/Sound is the Output.
  6. Challenge: Can you add a second light? What happens to the brightness?
