Chapter 8: Building Your Own Models

NGSS Standards: (No specific NGSS - this is a modeling skills chapter)

Opening Hook

Scientists don't just run simulations—they BUILD them. Every model you have explored was designed by someone who asked: "How does this system work?" Now it's your turn to be the model builder.

The Design Process

Building a scientific model is a creative process that requires careful planning. Before you ever touch a computer, you need to understand what you are trying to represent.

Defining the Question

Every good model starts with a question. A clear question helps you decide what is important to include and what can be left out.
  • Too broad: "How does an ecosystem work?"
  • Just right: "How does the number of wolves affect the deer population?"

Sketching Relationships

Once you have your question, grab a piece of paper and sketch it out. Draw circles for the main parts and lines connecting them. This "concept map" is the blueprint for your model.
Design Tip: Start Simple! Don't try to include everything in the world. Start with the most important parts. You can always add more detail later. This is called abstraction—focusing only on the relevant details.

Identifying Components

The parts that make up your system are called components.
Component: A distinct part or element of a larger system. In a computer model, these are often the "nodes" or "agents."
When listing your components, try to categorize them:
  • Living things: Animals, plants, bacteria
  • Non-living resources: Water, sunlight, nutrients
  • States or Conditions: Temperature, pH, infection status (healthy vs. sick)
Variable: A component or factor that can change or vary over time.

Creating Relationships and Interactions

Components in a system don't just sit there; they affect each other. These connections are called interactions or relationships.
Interaction: The action or influence of people, groups, or things on one another.
Relationship: The way in which two or more concepts, objects, or people are connected.
When drawing connections (usually with arrows), ask yourself:
  • Does A increase B? (Positive relationship)
  • Does A decrease B? (Negative relationship)
  • Is the relationship one-way or two-way?
Common Mistake to Avoid: Don't confuse "eating" with "energy flow." If a wolf eats a rabbit, the rabbit population goes DOWN, but the wolf's energy goes UP. Make sure your model arrows represent the correct flow of influence!

Adding Regulatory Elements

Systems are rarely simple straight lines. They have loops and switches that control them. This is called regulation.
Regulation: The control or maintenance of a process or rate.

Feedback Loops

Positive Feedback: A process that amplifies or increases change (e.g., population explosion).
Negative Feedback: A process that counteracts change and maintains stability (e.g., a thermostat maintaining temperature).

Thresholds and Switches

Sometimes a relationship only happens if a certain condition is met. For example, a fire might only start if the temperature is above a certain threshold.
Think Like a Scientist: Nature is full of negative feedback loops that keep things balanced (homeostasis). If your model crashes or explodes to infinity instantly, you might be missing a negative feedback loop!

Testing and Refining Your Model

Once you've built your model, you need to see if it works. This process is called validation.
Validation: The process of checking if a model accurately represents the real system.

Running Simulations

Run your simulation and watch what happens.
  • Does the behavior match your expectations?
  • Does it match data from the real world?
  • If you change a variable, does the outcome make sense?
Hypothesis: A proposed explanation made on the basis of limited evidence as a starting point for further investigation. Your model is a way to test a hypothesis!

Iterating

Your first model will almost never be perfect. That's okay! Science is about iteration.
Iteration: The process of repeating steps to refine and improve a design or model.
If your model doesn't work, go back to the design process. Did you miss a component? Is a relationship backwards? Make changes and test again.

Sharing Your Work with the Class

Science is a collaborative effort. Once you have a working model, you need to communicate your findings.

Peer Review

Show your model to a classmate. Can they understand it without you explaining every step?
  • Explain your question and hypothesis.
  • Walk them through your components and relationships.
  • Show a simulation run and explain the results.
Communication Tip: Use clear labels and colors. A messy model is hard to understand. Group related components together to make it easier to read.

Vocabulary

Component: A distinct part or element of a larger system
Interaction: The action or influence of people, groups, or things on one another
Relationship: The way in which two or more concepts, objects, or people are connected
Positive Feedback: A loop that amplifies change, moving a system away from equilibrium
Negative Feedback: A loop that counteracts change, helping to maintain stability
Regulation: The control or maintenance of a rate or process
Validation: Checking if a model accurately represents the real world
Iteration: Repeating a process to improve the result
Hypothesis: A testable prediction or explanation
Variable: A factor that can change in an experiment or model

HANDS-ON ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Model a Simple Feedback Loop

Learning Objective: Understand how negative feedback maintains balance.

Materials: Cell Collective or paper/pencil

Time Required: 20 minutes

Steps:

  1. Identify your goal: Maintain a comfortable room temperature (e.g., 70°F).
  2. Components: Room Temperature, Heater, Thermostat Setting.
  3. Relationships:
    • Heater ON increases Room Temperature.
    • Room Temperature > Thermostat Setting turns Heater OFF.
    • Room Temperature < Thermostat Setting turns Heater ON.
  4. Build this logic into your model.
  5. Run the simulation. Does the temperature stabilize or fluctuate wildly?
  6. Challenge: Add "Outside Cold Air" that constantly lowers the Room Temperature. How does the system respond?

Activity 2: Create an Ecosystem Model

Learning Objective: Model trophic interactions and population dynamics.

Materials: Cell Collective

Time Required: 40 minutes

Steps:

  1. Choose 3-4 species (e.g., Grass, Rabbit, Fox).
  2. Components: Add a node for each species.
  3. Interactions: Draw arrows to show who eats whom.
    • Grass increases Rabbit population.
    • Rabbit increases Fox population.
    • Rabbit decreases Grass quantity.
    • Fox decreases Rabbit population.
  4. Add a resource limit for the grass (e.g., Sun/Rain).
  5. Run the simulation. Can you get all three species to survive together?
  6. What happens if you remove the Foxes?

Activity 3: Build a Disease Spread Model

Learning Objective: Simulate how infection spreads through a population.

Materials: Cell Collective

Time Required: 40 minutes

Steps:

  1. Components: Healthy People, Infected People, Recovered People.
  2. Relationships:
    • Healthy People + Infected People -> More Infected People (Transmission).
    • Infected People -> Recovered People (Recovery over time).
  3. Set initial values: Start with 99 Healthy and 1 Infected.
  4. Run the simulation. Watch the curve of infection.
  5. Experiment: Add a "Social Distancing" switch that reduces the transmission rate. How does this flatten the curve?
