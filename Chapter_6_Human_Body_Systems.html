Chapter 6: Human Body Systems

NGSS Standards: MS-LS1-3, MS-LS1-8

Opening Hook

Right now, your heart is pumping blood, your lungs are breathing air, and your stomach is digesting food—all without you thinking about it. How do billions of individual cells work together as ONE unified system?

Overview of Body Systems

Your body is a complex machine made up of several major organ systems. There are 11 main systems in total, and they all work together to keep you alive and healthy.

The Team Players

Just like a sports team needs different players with different skills (goalie, forward, defender), your body needs different systems to perform different functions:
  • Structural Systems: Skeletal, Muscular, Integumentary (skin)
  • Control Systems: Nervous, Endocrine
  • Transport Systems: Circulatory, Lymphatic
  • Maintenance Systems: Respiratory, Digestive, Urinary
  • Reproductive System: For creating offspring
Amazing Fact: Your body contains about 37 trillion cells! That's more than the number of stars in the Milky Way galaxy. All these cells must cooperate perfectly for you to survive.

The Circulatory System

The Circulatory system is your body's transportation network. It moves blood, nutrients, oxygen, and waste throughout your body.

The Engine: The Heart

Your Heart is a powerful muscular pump about the size of your fist. It beats about 100,000 times a day!
The heart has two sides: the right side pumps blood to the lungs to get oxygen, and the left side pumps oxygen-rich blood to the rest of the body.

The Roadways: Blood Vessels

Blood travels through three main types of tubes:
  • Arteries: Carry blood AWAY from the heart. They have thick walls to handle high pressure.
  • Veins: Carry blood BACK to the heart. They have valves to keep blood flowing in one direction.
  • Capillaries: Tiny vessels connecting arteries and veins. This is where oxygen and nutrients are delivered to cells.

The Respiratory System

Your Respiratory system is responsible for breathing—bringing oxygen in and getting carbon dioxide out.

Breathing Mechanics

The main organs are your Lungs, but they can't move on their own. They rely on a large muscle called the Diaphragm.
  • Inhale: Diaphragm moves down, chest expands, air rushes in.
  • Exhale: Diaphragm moves up, chest shrinks, air pushes out.
Real-World Example: Think of your lungs like balloons inside a bottle. When you pull the bottom of the bottle (the diaphragm) down, the balloons inflate!

The Digestive System

The Digestive system breaks down food into nutrients your cells can use for energy.

The Journey of Food

  1. Mouth: Teeth crush food and saliva starts breaking it down chemically.
  2. Esophagus: Tube that carries food to the stomach.
  3. Stomach: Muscular bag that mixes food with strong acid.
  4. Small Intestine: Where most digestion happens and nutrients are absorbed into the blood.
  5. Large Intestine: Absorbs water and creates waste.
The Intestines are incredibly long—if you stretched out your small intestine, it would be about 20 feet long!

The Nervous System

The Nervous system is the control center. It sends and receives electrical messages throughout the body.

Parts of the System

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): The Brain and Spinal Cord. The brain is the boss, and the spinal cord is the main highway for messages.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Nerves that branch out to every part of your body.

How Messages Travel

Messages travel through specialized cells called Neurons.
Between neurons, there is a tiny gap called a Synapse. Chemical signals jump across this gap to pass the message along.

Systems Working Together

None of these systems work alone. They must collaborate to maintain Homeostasis—a stable internal environment.

Teamwork Examples

  • Running a Race:
    • Nervous system signals muscles to move.
    • Respiratory system breathes faster to get more oxygen.
    • Circulatory system pumps heart faster to deliver that oxygen to muscles.
    • Digestive system slows down to save energy.
  • Eating Lunch:
    • Digestive system breaks down food.
    • Circulatory system picks up nutrients from intestines and delivers them to cells.
    • Endocrine system (hormones) tells cells to store sugar.
Feedback Loops: Your body uses feedback loops to stay balanced. If you get too hot, you sweat to cool down. If you get too cold, you shiver to warm up. This is homeostasis in action!

Vocabulary

Circulatory: System that transports blood, nutrients, and oxygen through the body
Heart: Muscular organ that pumps blood throughout the body
Arteries: Blood vessels that carry blood away from the heart
Veins: Blood vessels that carry blood back to the heart
Capillaries: Tiny blood vessels where nutrient and gas exchange occurs
Respiratory: System responsible for taking in oxygen and expelling carbon dioxide
Lungs: Main organs of the respiratory system where gas exchange happens
Diaphragm: Muscle below the lungs that controls breathing
Digestive: System that breaks down food into absorbable nutrients
Stomach: Organ that chemically and mechanically breaks down food
Intestines: Organs (small and large) that digest food and absorb nutrients/water
Nervous: System that controls the body by sending electrical signals
Neurons: Specialized nerve cells that transmit information
Synapse: The tiny gap between neurons where signals are transmitted
Homeostasis: The process of maintaining a stable internal environment

HANDS-ON ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Measure Heart Rate and Breathing

Learning Objective: Observe how body systems respond to exercise

Materials: Stopwatch or clock with second hand, graph paper

Time Required: 30 minutes

Steps:

  1. Find your resting pulse (wrist or neck) and count beats for 1 minute.
  2. Count your breaths for 1 minute while sitting still.
  3. Do jumping jacks or run in place for 2 minutes.
  4. Immediately measure your pulse and breathing rate again.
  5. Rest for 2 minutes and measure again.
  6. Graph your results: Time vs. Heart Rate/Breathing Rate.
  7. Explain: How did your circulatory and respiratory systems work together?

Activity 2: Digestion Simulation

Learning Objective: Model mechanical and chemical digestion

Materials: Ziploc bag, cracker or bread, orange juice (acid), water, bowl

Time Required: 20 minutes

Steps:

  1. Place a cracker in a Ziploc bag (this represents the stomach).
  2. Add a splash of orange juice (represents stomach acid) and water (saliva).
  3. Seal the bag tightly.
  4. Smash the bag with your hands (mechanical digestion like the stomach muscles).
  5. Observe how the solid food turns into a liquid mush (chyme).
  6. Discuss: Which parts represented mechanical digestion vs. chemical digestion?

Activity 3: Reaction Time Test

Learning Objective: Measure neural response time

Materials: Ruler (30cm), partner

Time Required: 15 minutes

Steps:

  1. Student A holds the ruler vertically at the top (near 30cm mark).
  2. Student B places fingers ready to catch at the bottom (0cm mark) without touching.
  3. Student A drops the ruler without warning.
  4. Student B catches it as fast as possible.
  5. Record the measurement where fingers caught the ruler. Lower number = faster reaction.
  6. Repeat 3 times and average the score. Compare with classmates.
  7. Explain: Trace the path of the signal from eyes to brain to fingers.
